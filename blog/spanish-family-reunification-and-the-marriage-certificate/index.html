Spanish Family Reunification and the Marriage Certificate

Spain’s family reunification path runs through a translation requirement that catches most US filings, a freshness window that is rarely volunteered up front, and a consulate that does not interpret either of those rules loosely.

The reunification path in plain terms

Spanish reagrupación familiar allows a legally resident foreign national in Spain to bring a spouse, registered partner, dependent children, or dependent parents to join them under a derivative residency permit. For Americans, the most common case is a US-citizen spouse joining a Spanish resident, or a US permanent resident with Spanish family ties seeking the same path in reverse. The marriage certificate is the foundational document in either direction.

The path is not the non-lucrative visa. It is not the digital nomad visa. It is a separate category with its own documentary standards, its own consulate practice, and its own quirks that surprise applicants who prepared for one of the other Spanish visa paths and assumed the rules would be similar.

The translation requirement

Every US document submitted to Spanish authorities for family reunification must be translated into Spanish by a traductor jurado on the official register of the Spanish Ministry of Foreign Affairs. The register is public. The translators are credentialed by Spain. Their seal and signature on the translation are what give the document legal standing in the Spanish immigration system.

US-based certified translation services, even those marketed as suitable for Spanish use, generally do not satisfy this requirement. The Spanish consulate evaluating the file is checking for a traductor jurado seal, not a US translator’s certification statement. Files that arrive with US-certified translations are returned for proper sworn translation, and the file does not progress until the corrected translation is submitted.

This applies to the marriage certificate itself and to the apostille certificate attached to it. The translation must cover both. Marriage certificates translated correctly but with the apostille left in English are routinely returned. The translator has to handle the full document, end to end.

The freshness rule

Spanish family reunification applies a freshness rule on the marriage certificate. The consulate practice is variable, but six months from the date the certified copy was issued is the most common standard. Some consulates accept up to a year. Three-month windows are rare in family reunification contexts but have been observed.

The freshness clock runs from the date the state vital records authority issued the certified copy, not from the apostille date and not from the wedding date. A couple married fifteen years ago can pull a fresh certified copy today, have it apostilled this month, and the document satisfies the freshness rule. The original wedding date is irrelevant. What matters is when the state re-issued the certified copy.

This is one of the more counterintuitive aspects of the rule. Couples often assume that a long-married marriage cannot satisfy a “recent” certificate requirement, when in fact recent state issuance is the entire point.

The translator-on-the-register problem

Locating a traductor jurado willing to handle a US marriage certificate translation on a reasonable timeline is sometimes harder than the apostille step itself. The translators on the Spanish register are individuals, not agencies. Many work full-time on Spanish legal matters and treat US-document translation as occasional work. Some specialize in it and are responsive. Others quote turnaround times of four to six weeks, which can collapse the available window for a six-month freshness rule.

The practical advice: line up the translator before pulling the certificate, not after. Confirm availability, confirm format expectations, confirm whether the translator will translate the apostille along with the certificate (some need to be asked explicitly), and confirm delivery method. Some Spanish consulates accept digital sworn translations with electronic signature; others require paper originals shipped from Spain. The translator and the consulate need to be aligned on which.

Where the chain compounds

The marriage certificate apostille is one document in a reunification packet that typically includes proof of the sponsor’s legal residency in Spain, proof of stable income or financial means, proof of adequate housing, the dependent’s passport, dependent passport photos, the FBI background check apostille (for adult applicants), and the marriage certificate apostille with sworn translation.

Each foreign-use document in this packet follows the same Spanish translation rule. Each one needs a traductor jurado. The translation cost compounds across the file, and the time required to coordinate translations across multiple documents is the most underestimated variable in the family reunification timeline.

Where filings stall

The reunification cases that stall on marriage certificate issues tend to share patterns. The certificate was pulled years ago for another purpose and fails the freshness rule. The certificate was translated in the US and the consulate returns it for proper sworn translation. The certificate is correct but the apostille on it was issued by the wrong authority (a state Secretary of State for a federal-issued document, or vice versa). The certificate is correct and properly authenticated but the children’s birth certificates supporting the reunification claim were overlooked.

The pattern, as with most Spanish visa categories, is that the consulate does not interpret rules loosely. Documents that almost meet the requirement are not accepted. The file either is complete or it is not.
